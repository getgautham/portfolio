Synchronise AI

An AI employee for small businesses. It watches the tools a business already runs, drafts the work, and stages it for approval. The owner approves; the server sends. No agent can send, post, or spend on its own.

The problem

Small businesses want the work done, not another dashboard.

Owners of small businesses are drowning in tools: email, payments, accounting, ads, bookings. AI can do a lot of that work, but the moment software can act on its own, trust collapses. Nobody wants an agent quietly sending an email to a customer or spending money.

The design challenge: make AI feel like a capable employee you'd actually hand work to, where the human stays in control by default, and control never feels like friction.

The pattern

Read, edit, approve. The agent drafts; the person decides.

The core interaction is a work-queue card. The agent does the work and shows it fully drafted, with its source and a risk read. The person stays the editor: read it, tweak it, approve it. Only approval triggers the server to act.

Human-in-the-loop isn't a setting buried in preferences here. It's the shape of the whole product, and the design is what makes that shape feel like a superpower instead of a chore. Try it:

Brand as a variable

The accent is never hardcoded.

Every workspace should feel unmistakably like the business using it, without a designer touching each one. So the interaction color is a slot, not a constant: derived per customer from their own brand color and luminance-guarded, so it stays legible on any hue while the interface stays coherent.

This is the closest thing I've built to brands managing their identity inside software, which is exactly why the demo above is here. Change the color; the product stays itself, and stays theirs.

The design system

One system, not twelve.

The app had drifted until every tab read like a different product: roughly twelve one-off button classes, two monospaces, hand-typed padding, a hardcoded accent fighting the per-workspace brand. I authored the system that pulled it back together.

  • One button system, one type scale, one accent, replacing about twelve bespoke classes.
  • Depth built from surface steps and hairlines, not drop shadows.
  • A warm, light-first surface with the operator voice carried by a single monospace.
  • Exactly one of everything. Consistency as a design decision, not a cleanup task.
Research

Over 100 cold calls, turned into design decisions.

I ran more than 100 cold calls to small-business owners, booked demos, and turned them into user interviews. The finding wasn't abstract: owners want the work done, but they will not let software act unwatched.

That's exactly where the risk tiers and the read / edit / approve flow came from. I did the outreach, the interviews, and the design decisions myself, so nothing got lost in translation.
